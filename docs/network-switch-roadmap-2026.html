Switch roadmap for a low-power 10G core

A multi-year migration plan that keeps the UDM Pro as the router, keeps the office Flex, replaces the US16 access layer deliberately, and moves storage traffic off the UDM switch path.

Recommendation

Buy a USW-Aggregation as the core first, then replace the US16 with USW-Pro-Max-16-PoE if four 2.5GbE ports are enough.

Do not solve this by making the UDM Pro the centre of east-west traffic. Do not buy a 24-port switch just because it looks like the grown-up member of the family. Your actual current port count is below 16; the issue is 2.5G scarcity, not total port scarcity.

The confrontation

Your hardest conflict

You want US16-style power resilience, a non-UDM backbone, multigig access, low power, rack-coherent hardware, sub-£1000 cost, and fewer than 16 used ports. You cannot optimize all of those at once. The painful tradeoff is 2.5G port count: the 16-port replacement only gives you four 2.5GbE ports.

The decision you should make explicitly

If the US16 is being removed, stop pretending its RPS behavior survives. The Pro Max 16 PoE uses an external power adapter and does not give you the same USP-RPS-backed story. The 24-port Pro Max models have USP RPS DC input; the 16-port Pro Max models do not.

The UDM Pro point is correct, with a catch

Storage traffic should stay on the switching core. If your Mac and UNAS are in different routed VLANs, the UDM Pro becomes the critical path again. Put high-throughput storage clients and UNAS on a design where their large flows switch at L2 unless you have a real L3 core ready.

The US16 is not bad, but it is redundant

Once a Pro Max PoE access switch is installed, keeping the US16 means paying idle watts for nostalgia and backup convenience. Retire or sell it after the new access switch is stable, unless the RPS behavior is worth the extra power draw to you.

RPS and rack reality

Pro Max 16 PoE

No USP-RPS input. It uses an external AC/DC adapter. Treat it as UPS-backed only, not RPS-backed.

Rack mounting

The switch body is 325.1 x 160 x 43.7 mm, so it is not a normal full-width 19" chassis. Use the UACC-Pro-Max-16-RM rack-mount accessory when available, or a proper shelf/adapter.

RPS decision

Phase out RPS for access switching if the priority is lower idle power and fewer boxes. Keep RPS only for the UDM Pro and any future full-width core device where router/core uptime justifies the hardware.

Simpler Less maintenance resilience

Implementation plan

Phase 1: 10G core

Install USW-Aggregation in the rack.

Connect UDM Pro SFP+ LAN to aggregation at 10G.

Move UNAS Pro 10G to aggregation.

Move office Flex uplink to aggregation using UACC-CM-RJ45-MG over Cat6.

Connect US16 to aggregation at 1G for PoE/legacy access.

Phase 2: 2.5G access

Replace the US16 with USW-Pro-Max-16-PoE.

Connect the three Radxa nodes at 2.5G.

Use the fourth 2.5G PoE++ port deliberately: pick either U7 Pro or U7 In-Wall. The 16-port switch does not have enough multigig ports for three Radxa nodes plus both U7-class APs.

Put U6 LR, U6 Mesh, and low-throughput PoE devices on 1G PoE+ ports.

Phase 3: cleanup

Keep U6-Mesh, but stop treating anything behind its mesh backhaul as performance infrastructure.

Sell the spare U6 Lite if coverage is already solved; it adds management and power overhead without moving the bottleneck.

Bring the U7 In-Wall online only if it replaces a worse AP/client location or provides useful 2.5G wired edge access.

Validation

After each phase, verify negotiated links in UniFi.

Run a MacBook Pro to UNAS test before and after: Time Machine is not a pure link-speed benchmark, so also test with `iperf3` or a large sequential file copy.

Watch switch temperature and total rack watts for a week before buying the next phase.

Garage backhaul

Permanent physical constraint

Do not plan new Ethernet or fibre between house and garage. Garden constraints make physical house-to-garage cabling unavailable, so the garage path must be solved with wireless backhaul or existing infrastructure.

Why U7 Pro Outdoor is the serious AP option

The garage link is a directional path problem, not a normal room coverage problem. The U7 Pro Outdoor has outdoor housing, a 2.5GbE uplink, WiFi 7, 6 GHz support, and stronger directional antennas than the indoor U7 Pro. That makes it a better house-side bridge candidate than another ceiling disc.

Option Backhaul value Verdict
U6 LR Strong existing WiFi 6 indoor AP with 4 x 4 5 GHz, but only a 1GbE uplink and an indoor ceiling-style radiation pattern. Keep unless the garage link is poor. It is not the weakest part just because it is older.
U7 LR 2.5GbE and WiFi 7, but still an indoor long-range AP. It improves client AP capability more than it solves a directional outbuilding path. Not my first choice for the garage backhaul.

Power notes

Every continuous 10W is 87.6kWh per year. At a rough UK electricity range of £0.25-£0.30/kWh, that is about £22-£26 per year per 10W. This is why I am pushing against the Pro XG as the first move: its 61W excluding PoE output is technically lovely and operationally expensive.

The recommended path adds capability where it matters first. It does not pretend that keeping the US16 gives the new core redundant power. If switch-core uptime during UPS maintenance is non-negotiable, the budget or power target has to move.

The access recommendation now replaces the US16 instead of adding another large switch beside it. The Pro Max 16 PoE keeps PoE in one box and avoids the £806.40 Pro Max 24 PoE, but the cost is real: only four ports are multigig.

The RPS should not drive the access-switch decision anymore. It made sense when the US16 was already there and doing useful PoE work. Once the replacement is a Pro Max 16 PoE, preserving RPS means jumping to larger, more expensive, higher-power hardware to solve a maintenance edge case. Keep the router/core on UPS and RPS where supported; let access be UPS-backed.
